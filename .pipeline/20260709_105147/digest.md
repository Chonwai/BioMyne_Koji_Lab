### Odor Annoyance, Sensory Irritation or Relaxation: Acute Effects of Real Pinewood Emissions in Indoor Air Scenarios
**Priority:** low | **Tags:** indoor air quality, occupational health, VOC exposure, human physiology
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.03.736270v1

A study by researchers at the Leibniz Research Centre for Working Environment, IfADo (Federal Medical Advisory Service), and the Thuenen Institute of Wood Research examined whether pinewood volatile organic compound (VOC) emissions at two German guide-value concentrations (GV I: 0.2 mg/m3 and GV II: 2.0 mg/m3 for bicyclic terpenes) produce odour annoyance, sensory irritation, or relaxation effects. Thirty-two healthy participants completed a 2-hour exposure study using a within-subject design that included a demanding n-back working memory task, a resting period with heart rate (HR) and HR variability (HRV) monitoring, and pre- and post-session physiological irritation markers. Contrary to expectations of pleasant, relaxing wood odours, ratings showed no distinct or more intense/pinewood-relevant odour at either concentration; inter-individual factors dominated over VOC-driven effects, the pinewood odor neither distracted nor facilitated cognitive performance during the n-back task, and HR/HRV changes indicated no relaxation response post-exposure. Only mild nasal irritation symptoms suggested weak concentration-dependence, unsupported by physiological markers or matching subjective ratings—the authors conclude that the absence of a distinct odour at relevant indoor air concentrations suggests interfering factors may prevent meaningful regulation of wood-derived odours at these levels.

### Scanning transcriptomes for nonlinear, domain-level similarities using hmSEEKR
**Priority:** medium | **Tags:** non-coding RNA, computational biology, gene regulation
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.03.736302v1

Researchers at UNC Chapel Hill (Shuang Li, J Mauro Calabrese, Alain Laederach and colleagues) have developed hmSEEKR, a k-mer-based hidden Markov model that scans whole transcriptomes for regions of nonlinear sequence similarity to any RNA domain query without requiring prior knowledge of target locations. Applying the tool to well-characterized lncRNAs, they showed that individual domains within XIST, NEAT1, and MALAT1 show widely varying degrees of regional similarity across the transcriptome, with some matching only ~100 or fewer genes and others showing broad matches. Combinatorial searches for RNAs containing sequential matches to core functional domains of XIST and NEAT1 recovered protein-interaction networks (inferred from eCLIP data) that more closely resembled those of the reference lncRNAs than expected by chance, suggesting the method identifies functionally related transcripts. In annotated sets of cis-activating and cis-repressive lncRNAs, they found opposing enrichments for similarity to transcription-promoting complex domains against heterogeneous nuclear ribonucleoprotein (hnRNP) binding domains, implicating these sequences in regulatory function. The work is significant as an open computational approach enabling functional inference for noncoding RNAs with minimal training data, potentially accelerating lncRNA target and mechanism discovery for biotech pipeline development.

### Chronic Hypoxic Signalling Reprograms Metabolism and Alters Redox and Lipid Homeostasis in Rod Photoreceptors
**Priority:** medium | **Tags:** metabolism, retinal disease, HIF pathway, lipid metabolism, redox biology
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.03.736307v1

Researchers at the University of Zurich, Emory University, University of Oklahoma, and the University of Washington used a rod-specific Vhl knockout mouse (RodVhl) model with constitutive HIF activation to study how chronic hypoxic stress affects photoreceptor metabolism. Combining multi-omics with 13C-glucose metabolic flux tracing the team found an early oxidative redox shift in the retina characterized by decreased nucleotide precursors, accelerated carbon flow through three-carbon glycolytic intermediates, and selective phosphatidylcholine and phosphatidylethanolamine remodelling toward oxidation-resistant elongated acyl chains. Upregulation of fatty acid elongation desaturation and oxidative genes suggests this is a coordinated adaptive response meant to preserve outer segment integrity under hypoxic stress. The findings have direct relevance for understanding how age-related changes in oxygen delivery to the eye contribute to retinal degeneration including age-related macular degeneration and diabetic retinopathy and may inform therapeutic strategies targeting HIF pathways or metabolic modulation of photoreceptors.

### CICADA: A unified framework for NWB-based neurophysiological data analysis
**Priority:** low | **Tags:** neuroscience, computational-neuroscience
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.03.736318v1

A research team led by Robin F Dard and colleagues at EPFL and INSERM/Aix-Marseille University released CICADA, an open-source Python framework for analyzing neurophysiological data standardized in the Neurodata Without Borders (NWB) format. The framework is organized into three hierarchical packages: cicada-nwb provides the NWB access layer, cicada-analysis offers a plugin-based analysis engine and tool library, and cicada-gui delivers a PyQt5 desktop application as the pipeline frontend. By supporting both turnkey GUI-driven workflows and fully programmatic plugin pipelines, CICADA enables reproducible multi-session and multi-animal group analyses with identical behavior across interactive and headless modes. The authors demonstrated the framework on behavioral, calcium imaging (two-photon and widefield), and extracellular electrophysiology datasets from rodent experiments. This tool addresses a gap in extensible analysis frameworks built around the NWB standard, potentially making it a useful infrastructure component for computational neuroscience and neurotech pipelines.

### IgA plasma cells co-secrete monomeric and dimeric IgA
**Priority:** medium | **Tags:** immunology, antibody biology, plasma cells, mucosal immunity
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.03.736325v1

Researchers from Friedrich-Alexander-Universitaet Erlangen-Nuernberg, Aarhus University, and the University of Bonn used flow cytometry, a recombinant soluble PIGR binding assay, and single-cell DropMap secretion assays to demonstrate that IgA plasma cells from bone marrow, lymphoid, and mucosal tissues universally express intracellular JCHAIN and co-secrete both monomeric and dimeric IgA. These findings contradict the prevailing model that discrete plasma cell subsets are dedicated exclusively to producing either mIgA or dIgA, with bone marrow previously thought to be the primary source of monomeric IgA. The work establishes that dIgA assembly and secretion are a general property shared across all IgA plasma cell populations regardless of tissue origin. By generating a recombinant soluble PIGR tool that selectively binds JCHAIN-containing dIgA, the authors provide an improved approach for discriminating polymeric IgA in immunological assays. The findings carry significance for mucosal vaccine design, antibody-based therapeutics targeting secretory IgA pathways, and fundamentally reshaping the understanding of humoral immune responses at epithelial barriers.

### Integrin-engaged Cellular Patches Mechanically Impose a Mitochondrial Respiratory Bottleneck to Suppress Cancer Cell Motility
**Priority:** high | **Tags:** mechanobiology, cancer biology, metabolic reprogramming, integrin signaling
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.03.736349v1

Researchers from the Chinese Academy of Sciences (Dongguan Institute), City University of Hong Kong, Sun Yat-sen University, South China University of Technology, and the University of Tokyo report that clustering integrin-binding ligands on cell surfaces triggers a mechanotransduction pathway that constrains mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation capacity in tumor cells. In HeLa xenograft mice, this integrin-engaged supramolecular system suppressed tumor growth and reduced extracellular matrix deposition. The mitochondrial respiratory bottleneck directly limits cancer cell migration and invasion—a constraint not overcome by restoring cytoskeletal contractility but rescueable through replenishing mitochondrial metabolic substrates. The work establishes extracellular ligand organization as a new regulatory lever over cellular metabolism with anti-metastatic therapeutic potential. Three authors (Qizheng Zhang, Xunwu Hu, Ye Zhang) have filed a patent application on the technology.

### A chromosome-level reference genome of the largest cervid species - the European moose (Alces alces; Linnaeus, 1758)
**Priority:** low | **Tags:** genomics, wildlife-conservation
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.03.736352v1

A team at the University of Oslo and University of Bergen (Norway), funded by the Research Council of Norway, has produced a chromosome-level, haplotype-resolved reference genome assembly from a male European moose (Alces alces alces). The assembly consists of two pseudo-haplotypes measuring 3,148 Mb and 3,112 Mb, with sex chromosomes on haplotype one and 33 autosomes per haplotype (68 total). Assembly completeness is high at BUSCO scores of 98.3% and 95.7%, with 21,496 annotated protein-coding genes on haplotype one and 20,498 on haplotype two. This represents the most complete European moose genome assembled to date, providing a critical resource for wildlife genomics.

### Navigating the pangenome coordinate system with Shredtools
**Priority:** medium | **Tags:** bioinformatics, genomics
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.03.736354v1

Vikram S Shivakumar and Ben Langmead of Johns Hopkins University introduce Shredtools, a new computational tool that reimagines pangenome coordinate navigation by avoiding expensive multiple sequence alignments. Instead of relying on MSA-based coordinates, Shredtools uses multi-MUMs (multi-maximal unique matches) as pangenome waypoints to enable fast, sophisticated queries across reference-scale genomes. Its primary extract query takes an interval from one sequence and returns the smallest syntenic window spanning the entire pangenome—capable of extracting a gene region from 476 human genomes in approximately half a second. Additional enhance and zoom queries allow operators to improve multi-MUM density and refine syntenic precision by selectively discarding sequences. A web interface at vikshiv.github.io/shredtools provides client-side access with HTTP Range requests for index queries, lowering the barrier for pangenome-scale analysis.

### A voltage-step method for detecting high-frequency transient current components in deep brain tissue: preliminary in vivo measurements in rats
**Priority:** low | **Tags:** Biophysics, Neuroscience, Bioengineering
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.03.736373v1

Researchers at the University of Florida and Virginia Tech developed a voltage-step method to measure high-frequency transient current components in deep brain tissue using in vivo rat models. The technique applies a voltage pulse with high dV/dt to generate a corresponding current pulse, then analyzes frequency-domain responses via Fast Fourier Transform at 200 kHz sampling. The protocol was applied across 277 measurements in five Sprague Dawley rats implanted at cortical and basal ganglia electrode sites. Preliminary spectral results demonstrate measurable differences between catecholaminergic regions (rich in ferritin and neuromelanin) and surrounding cortical tissue, supporting the hypothesis that these iron- and melanin-containing structures may support specialized high-frequency electrical conduction. The work has no reported industry funding and represents a basic biophysics investigation rather than a clinical or commercial development.

### Ancient pyroptotic machinery via GSDMA/B cleavage by LPS-activated caspase-1 in cartilaginous fish
**Priority:** medium | **Tags:** immunology, evolutionary biology, pyroptosis, gasdermins, antimicrobial immunity
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.03.736391v1

Researchers at Sun Yat-sen University (Xuxia Wei, Shaochun Yuan, and co-authors) have characterized the functional origins of the GSDMA/B/C/D branch of gasdermins — pore-forming effector proteins that mediate pyroptosis, an inflammatory form of programmed cell death. Using the cartilaginous fish Callorhinchus milii (a chimaera or elephant fish) as a basal model for the GSDMA-D lineage, the team identified CmiCASP1 (caspase-1 ortholog) as the upstream protease that cleaves CmiGSDMA/B in response to lipopolysaccharide (LPS) signaling through its CARD domain. This non-canonical caspase-driven cleavage produces two functionally distinct fragments: N241, which binds the cell membrane to drive pyroptotic pore formation and exhibits direct bactericidal activity against Gram-negative bacteria in vitro; and N288, which acts as a dominant-negative regulator that suppresses N241-triggered cell death. Taken together, these findings establish a dual-fragment antagonistic regulatory mechanism within the GSDMA-D lineage and push the functional origins of this immune branch deeper into vertebrate evolution than previously documented. The discovery of in vitro bactericidal activity in N241 additionally suggests that antimicrobial effector functions — not just inflammatory cell death — may have been an ancestral feature of gasdermin-family proteins.

### Odors Smell Like Their Components: A Linear Framework for Predicting Olfactory Mixture Perception
**Priority:** high | **Tags:** computational neuroscience, olfactory science
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.03.736426v1

A research team including Peter Pellegrino, Emily J Mayhew, and colleagues at the Monell Chemical Senses Center and Osmo Labs demonstrated that olfactory mixture perception is largely linear, challenging the long-held assumption that odor-odor interactions produce unpredictable nonlinear effects. By testing 432 mixtures built from 144 distinct component odorants with a trained human panel, they found that a simple linear average of each component's quality profile accurately predicted the perceived character of nearly every mixture—including those previously cited as evidence for emergence. This framework significantly outperformed existing state-of-the-art models and reached within measurement noise of perfect prediction. The finding is analogous to how additive color mixing enabled quantitative colorimetry, but applied to smell: linear olfaction means mixtures can now be predicted, reconstructed, and optimized computationally rather than mapping exhaustive odor-odor interactions. For industry partners Osmo Labs (where coauthors ABW and RCG hold ownership and JDM serves on the scientific advisory board), this provides a computational foundation for precision fragrance formulation, flavor science, and odor-control technologies.

### A molecular integrator of sleep duration and interruption
**Priority:** high | **Tags:** neuroscience, molecular neuroscience, sleep physiology
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.03.736427v1

Research led by Elizabeth Tilden at Washington University in St. Louis identified PKA substrate phosphorylation (PKA-SP) as a molecular signal encoding within-bout sleep history — the first demonstration of any biochemical indicator tracking accumulated sleep across individual sleep episodes. Using real-time measurement in freely behaving mice, the team showed that membrane PKA-SP decreases exponentially within each sleep bout with consistent kinetics, integrating both total sleep duration and episode interruptions while continuously forecasting moment-to-moment waking probability. After sleep deprivation, PKA-SP reaches lower levels at bout termination, correlating with increased sleep need dissipation. This finding bridges fast cellular arousal circuits (operating on second timescales) with classical slow-wave homeostatic sleep drivers (acting over hours), resolving a long-standing gap in how the brain continuously monitors accumulated sleep need during ongoing sleep. The work was funded by NIH grants including R01-NS119821 and supported by the Mathers Foundation.

### Rapid coordination of followership and leadership roles in homing pigeons navigating with unfamiliar partners
**Priority:** low | **Tags:** animal behavior, collective intelligence
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.06.736763v1

Morford, Biro, and colleagues (University of Rochester, Oxford University, University of Leeds, Johns Hopkins) studied how pairs of homing pigeons coordinate collective navigation when flying together for the first time versus with familiar partners. Using bidirectional information flow analysis across repeated pair-wise releases followed by solo returns, they found that familiar pairs exhibited asymmetric information transfer on their first release—reflecting pre-existing leader-follower relationships—whereas unfamiliar pairs displayed symmetric exchange; this difference dissipated after a single flight. Familiarity had minimal effect on group cohesion or overall homing accuracy. There was modest evidence of learning: pigeons previously paired together homed more efficiently on a subsequent solo release, suggesting retained navigational knowledge from the shared experience. Across all partnerships, followership was more predictable than leadership with respect to individual identity and flight speed, indicating stable differences in individuals' tendencies to follow rather than lead. These findings suggest that shifting analytical emphasis from leadership toward followership may better capture collective decision-making dynamics.

### Spontaneous eye blinks as temporal markers of internal attention
**Priority:** low | **Tags:** neuroscience, cognitive science, BCI signals, digital biomarkers
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.06.736774v1

Researchers at the Leibniz Research Centre for Working Environment and Human Factors (Daniel Schneider, Sahcan Oezdemir, Edmund Wascher, Stefan Arnau) demonstrate that spontaneous eye blinks provide a measurable, time-resolved behavioural signal reflecting internal attentional states in working memory. In two experiments using EEG-locked analyses, blink timing was found to align with neural activity associated with focused attention on internal mental representations. Blink-frequency profiles shifted systematically depending on when the relevant visual information was revealed—increasing after early cues versus delayed probes—and these post-cue blinks correlated with improved memory performance. The findings establish that blink patterns can serve as an unobtrusive chronometric tool for tracking latent cognitive processing without requiring explicit participant reports.

### Conserved and specialized features of thalamocortical wiring revealed by single-cell projection mapping in mouse and marmoset
**Priority:** medium | **Tags:** neuroscience, brain mapping
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.07.736957v1

Researchers from the National Institute of Mental Health, Allen Institute, Johns Hopkins University, and Cold Spring Harbor Laboratory used in situ sequencing and BARseq projection mapping to characterize thalamocortical wiring at single-cell resolution in both marmosets and mice. The team profiled 1.5 million marmoset neurons and measured gene expression alongside cortical projections in 708 marmoset and 1,518 mouse thalamic neurons. They found that marmoset thalamocortical projections are more spatially segregated than mouse, producing a point-to-point architecture with local specialization. Despite this segregation, a conserved gradient across thalamic nuclei predominated over discrete anatomical borders in the higher-order sensory thalamus of both species, reconciling competing models of thalamic organization.

### Epitope Editing Strategy Could Enable Less Toxic Stem Cell Transplants
**Priority:** low | **Tags:** Genome Editing, Stem Cell Transplantation, Gene Therapy
**URL:** https://www.genengnews.com/topics/translational-medicine/epitope-editing-strategy-could-enable-less-toxic-stem-cell-transplants/

Researchers at Boston Children's Hospital and Dana-Farber Cancer Institute, led by PI Pietro Genovese, developed a preclinical epitope editing approach to replace DNA-damaging chemotherapy with targeted antibody-based bone marrow conditioning for hematopoietic stem/progenitor cell (HSPC) transplantation. Using precise genome editing of the KIT gene's extracellular domain, the team created molecular camouflage on donor stem cells that prevents therapeutic anti-CD117 monoclonal antibodies from binding and depleting them, while unedited patient stem cells remain vulnerable to depletion. The same editing approach co-targeted BCL11A to simultaneously increase fetal hemoglobin (HbF), providing dual benefit for sickle cell disease and beta-thalassemia. In mouse models, epitope-edited, HbF-elevated donor stem cells survived antibody-based conditioning, engrafted in bone marrow, and progressively enriched over time relative to unedited controls. The authors argue this could expand transplant access to patients with less severe diseases or marginal performance status currently excluded by chemo-radiation toxicity, including avoiding prolonged aplasia, infertility, and secondary malignancies as off-target effects.
