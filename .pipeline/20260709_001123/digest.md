### Spatial-EV-seq enables in situ spatial profiling of extracellular vesicle–cell communication networks
**Priority:** low | **Tags:** extracellular vesicles, spatial transcriptomics, cancer immunotherapy, PD-L1, biotech diagnostics
**URL:** https://www.nature.com/articles/s41587-026-03192-3

Researchers at Xiamen University and affiliated institutions have developed Spatial-EV-seq, a method for in situ spatial profiling of extracellular vesicles (EVs) that preserves their native microenvironmental distribution. The approach uses an antibody-engineered capture interface coupled with rolling circle amplification driven by EV surface-binding aptamers to enable fluorescence imaging and molecular subtyping of individual EVs. Applied to an anti-PD-1-treated 4T1 breast cancer mouse model, the method revealed a spatially orchestrated immunosuppressive axis: PDL1-positive EV-enriched zones drive CD8+ T cell dysfunction and establish immune-privileged niches, whereas PDL1-depleted regions retain immunocompetence and therapeutic sensitivity. By integrating spatial EV profiling with spatial transcriptomics of the same tissue sections, the team mapped location-specific EV-to-cell communication networks underlying resistance to checkpoint blockade.

### Light-powered metabolism in the mammalian eye
**Priority:** high | **Tags:** gene therapy, retinal disease, synthetic biology, oxidative stress, metabolic engineering
**URL:** https://www.nature.com/articles/s41587-026-03195-0

Xing et al. report transplanting isolated thylakoid grana—chloroplast compartments that drive light-dependent photosynthesis reactions—into mammalian eye cells to combat oxidative stress from a common eye disease. By encapsulating the thylakoids in the surfactant Pluronic F127, the team created LEAF (light-reaction enriched thylakoid NADPH-foundry), a nanosized system capable of producing NADPH without exogenous cofactors and independently of mammalian metabolic control. NADPH serves as an essential coenzyme and reducing agent that neutralizes oxidative damage—a primary driver of retinal degeneration in diseases such as age-related macular degeneration. This work represents the first demonstration of light-driven metabolic support in non-plant cells, opening a path toward optogenetic-inspired therapies that do not require genetic modification of target tissues. The approach could transform how biotech firms approach antioxidant-based treatments for retinal disease and broader oxidative stress disorders by using externally applied photosynthetic machinery as a drug replacement.

### Prime assembly uses a Gibson-like strategy for large DNA insertions
**Priority:** medium | **Tags:** gene editing, gene therapy
**URL:** https://www.nature.com/articles/s41587-026-03196-z

Liu et al. have developed prime assembly (PA), a gene editing system that overcomes key translational hurdles faced by existing prime editing tools—namely junctional scars at insertion sites and inflammatory responses triggered by plasmid-based donor templates. Building on twin prime editing, which generates ~30-nucleotide flaps on opposing DNA strands, PA introduces double-stranded DNA donors whose resected 5' ends anneal to those flaps in a Gibson assembly-like workflow. The system achieved up to 40% integration efficiency for a 0.8 kb donor in human cells, a marked improvement over prior prime editing approaches that struggled with large insertions. For biotech operators working on genetic disease therapy pipelines using gene editing, this represents a meaningful advance toward efficient, scar-free delivery of therapeutic-size DNA payloads without the immunogenicity risks of plasmid donors—though the preview does not reveal whether in vivo validation or animal models have been demonstrated.

### Unlocking the chemical potential of filamentous fungi using prime editing
**Priority:** high | **Tags:** genetic engineering, microbial genetics, genome editing, fungal biotechnology
**URL:** https://www.nature.com/articles/s41587-026-03219-9

Sun et al. report fPE7max, a prime editor optimized for filamentous fungi that enables precise genome engineering ranging from single-base substitutions to kilobase-scale insertions and multi-kilobase deletions across diverse fungal species. The team demonstrated that editing translation-regulatory elements—specifically upstream open reading frames (uORFs) in a pleiotropic metabolic regulator gene—can unlock previously inaccessible biosynthetic pathways. This approach uncovered new fungal metabolites that arise only after regulatory element disruption, expanding the tractable chemical space of fungal natural products. For biotech operators, the work means prime editing can replace traditional knockout or promoter-swap strategies to activate cryptic gene clusters in industrial filamentous fungi such as Aspergillus and Trichoderma, which are major hosts for enzyme and antibiotic production. The method is broadly applicable across fungal species and does not rely on double-strand breaks, reducing the indels that have historically limited editing efficiency in heterokaryotic molds.

### KOLF2.1J-Perturb: a genome-scale CRISPRi perturbation atlas of human iPSC single-cell transcriptomes
**Priority:** medium | **Tags:** CRISPR screening, stem cells, functional genomics, single-cell transcriptomics
**URL:** https://www.nature.com/articles/s41587-026-03199-w

A research team has constructed a comprehensive, expressed genome-scale CRISPR interference (CRISPRi) perturbation cell atlas using KOLF2.1J human induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs). The atlas maps transcriptional phenotypes resulting from perturbations of 11,692 genes across more than 2.5 million single cells, enabling reconstruction of a cell-state map for the pluripotent ground state that reveals functionally related protein complexes through phenotype correlation analysis. Using this resource, the authors discovered two key regulators — the metabolic factor ZBTB41 and pluripotency regulator RNF7 — and validated their biological functions via metabolic tracing, immunofluorescence, and protein-protein interaction assays. The atlas was deployed as an unbiased genome-wide screen for A-to-I RNA-editing modulators, identifying DBR1 (debranching enzyme) as a potent regulator, with functional validation performed. The dataset is publicly accessible as FASTQ files on BioProject PRJNA1173491, via a Figshare anndata file, and through an interactive web browser at y-doctor.github.io.

Nature Biotechnology published a piece on HySIL (hybrid solid–liquid optics), a new refractive framework for submicrometer-resolution 3D imaging of centimeter-scale biological samples. The technology replaces expensive, limited-working-distance immersion objectives with an index-matched optical design that leverages inexpensive standard air objectives across multiple immersion media. ### Quantum computing in transition
**Priority:** high | **Tags:** quantum computing, drug discovery, data security, biotech policy
**URL:** https://www.nature.com/articles/s41587-026-03233-x

A Nature Biotechnology perspective argues that near-term quantum advantage in biotech will come from hybrid quantum-classical systems, not fully fault-tolerant quantum machines. Concrete milestones include encoding the full hepatitis D virus genome on IBM quantum hardware and Q4Bio challenge results demonstrating protein complex simulations exceeding 12,000 atoms — the largest known simulations of biologically meaningful molecules to date. IBM predicts 2026 as the inflection point for practical quantum advantage via hybrid approaches, while Google's Willow chip has demonstrated sub-threshold error rates enabling exponential logical error suppression. The article warns that sensitive genomic data faces 'harvest now, decrypt later' quantum cryptanalysis threats, and post-quantum cryptographic standards from NIST (2024) urgently need adoption in biological databases. Finally, the piece flags a 'quantum divide': without deliberate global initiatives like UNESCO's proposal, early quantum advantage will accrue only to well-funded U.S., European, and Chinese institutions, neglecting tropical disease research and antimicrobial challenges in low-income settings.
