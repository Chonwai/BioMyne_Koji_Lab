### In vitro evaluation of the activity of pairwise combinations of zoliflodacin, gepotidacin, and ciprofloxacin against Neisseria gonorrhoeae
**Priority:** high | **Tags:** antibiotics, anti-infectives, drug-resistance, pharmacology
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.07.737061v1

Researchers at the Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health performed in vitro checkerboard assays evaluating the pairwise drug interactions among zoliflodacin, gepotidacin, and ciprofloxacin against Neisseria gonorrhoeae. The study found no evidence of antagonism across all six pairwise combinations tested, including in isolates with elevated ciprofloxacin minimum inhibitory concentrations (MICs). This finding supports the safe co-administration of these three antibiotics for urogenital gonorrhea treatment and provides preclinical evidence that combo regimens using zoliflodacin or gepotidacin with fluoroquinolone therapy are unlikely to be counterproductive. With both zoliflodacin and gepotidacin recently approved, this work is directly relevant to GSK’s gonorrhea pipeline in designing combination treatment protocols that delay resistance emergence. The absence of antagonism gives clinicians and public health programs additional confidence in flexible, multi-drug gonorrhea treatment strategies.

### Narrow-beam geometry improves the efficiency of cryo-EM
**Priority:** medium | **Tags:** cryo-EM, structural biology
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.06.736854v1

Researchers from the University of Basel and Paul Scherrer Institute report that narrowing the electron beam in cryo-electron microscopy substantially reduces radiation damage and improves signal-to-noise ratio in biological specimens. The team demonstrated that reducing illuminated area slows diffraction decay in protein microcrystals, and that narrow parallel-beam electron diffraction yields higher reciprocal-space SNR than conventional cryo-EM imaging. They developed a multimodal scanning workflow called 4D-para-STEM that simultaneously records narrow-beam diffraction patterns alongside corresponding images. The approach was validated across multiple specimen types including viruses, peptide assemblies, and microtubules, producing interpretable diffraction signals in both crystalline and non-crystalline samples. These findings offer a practical methodological advance for structural biology labs relying on cryo-EM to resolve protein structures at near-atomic resolution.

### Obstacle bypassing and intrinsically asymmetric loop extrusion in the segment-capture model of Structural Maintenance of Chromosomes complexes
**Priority:** low | **Tags:** biophysics, chromosome-architecture
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.07.736920v1

The authors present theoretical work defending and extending the segment-capture model of DNA loop extrusion by Structural Maintenance of Chromosomes (SMC) complexes, which operate as molecular motors that reel inDNA to form loops. They address prior experimental objections that SMCs cannot bypass large nanoparticle obstacles on DNA, demonstrating computationally that such bypass occurs naturally when the SMC hinge protein serves as a bypass gate. The paper outlines a set of targeted experiments designed to critically test model predictions, providing a concrete roadmap for resolving outstanding questions in loop extrusion mechanics. This work was conducted by Lucas Dooms, Stefanos K Nomidis, and Enrico Carlon at KU Leuven, with Stephan Gruber (University of Lausanne) and John F Marko (Northwestern University), and funded by FWO, NIH (R35-GM161403), and SNSF.

### Spatial Variability in Soil Necrobiome Communities has a Negligible Effect on Postmortem Interval Estimation
**Priority:** low | **Tags:** forensic microbiology, microbial ecology
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.07.737041v1

Researchers at Trent University and the University of Windsor investigated whether spatial variation within cadaver decomposition islands (CDIs) compromises microbial-based postmortem interval (PMI) estimation, a critical forensic application. They collected soil samples across multiple anatomical locations from two human body donors' CDIs and characterized necrobiome communities using 16S rRNA sequencing combined with statistical modelling. Necrobiome community metrics showed no significant differences across sampling sites at any single timepoint, and temporal modelling identified 11 taxa with significant PMI relationships in one donor despite spatial heterogeneity. Non-linear approaches similarly revealed PMI-correlated taxa in the second donor. The study demonstrates that opportunistic (non-stratified) soil sampling can reliably capture robust linear and non-linear PMI signals even in later decomposition stages.

### The Blood RNA Stability Atlas: defining temporal structure and trait-state programs in the human whole-blood transcriptome
**Priority:** high | **Tags:** biomarkers, transcriptomics
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.07.737044v1

Baltazar, Messing, and Ferguson at Baylor College of Medicine, University of Texas at Austin, and UNC Chapel Hill analyzed 968 longitudinal whole-blood transcriptomes from 165 healthy donors across eight independent studies spanning time scales from 50 minutes to 16 weeks. They quantified temporal stability for 6,064 RNAs and classified them into 1,118 trait RNAs (stable within-individual abundance but variable between individuals, enriched for whole-blood eGenes) and 1,504 state RNAs (context-dependent temporal variation enriched for translation and RNA-binding pathways). Integration with genetic datasets revealed that 72% of blood transcripts (4,395 of 6,064) are linked to at least one whole-blood eQTL and associate with 18,358 GWAS trait relationships. The Blood RNA Atlas provides an interactive, publicly accessible framework combining top-down (disease-to-gene) and bottom-up (gene-to-context) approaches to help researchers distinguish stable baseline transcripts from context-dependent transcriptional responses before designing biomarker-driven clinical studies.

### Beyond Bisulfite Sequencing: Resolving 5-hmC with Nanopore Sequencing Unmasks the True-5mC Methylation Entropy Landscape
**Priority:** medium | **Tags:** epigenetics, methylation analysis, nanopore sequencing, cancer biology, DNA methylation
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.08.736699v1

Researchers at Tel Aviv University and The Hebrew University of Jerusalem developed a nanopore sequencing approach that directly distinguishes between 5-methylcytosine (5mC) and 5-hydroxymethylcytosine (5hmC), addressing a fundamental limitation of bisulfite sequencing which conflates both as identical 5mC signals. Applying this method to kidney cancer samples and mouse medial prefrontal cortex tissue, the study found that conventional bisulfite-like analysis introduced systematic, tissue-specific shifts in methylation distributions that altered biological interpretation. However, these discrepancies were modest in low-5hmC kidney cancer samples where pathway-level results remained highly concordant. The key finding is that true 5mC-based methylation entropy redefines the physical mapping of epigenomes: signals historically attributed to stochastic maintenance failure are often structured signatures of distinct cytosine biochemistry with mechanistic interpretability. This work has implications for aging research, cancer epigenomics, and any field relying on DNA methylation profiling as a biomarker or mechanistic readout.

### SpoIVA contributes to efficient engulfment through a cytoskeletal-like mechanism during Bacillus subtilis sporulation
**Priority:** low | **Tags:** microbiology, bacterial-sporulation, structural-biology, cytoskeletal-mechanisms
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.08.736982v1

Researchers at the University of Warwick and CNRS report that SpoIVA, an ATPase protein essential for endospore development in Bacillus subtilis, plays a mechanical structural role during bacterial sporulation beyond its known function as a coat layer basement. Using a spoIVA null mutant (ΔspoIVA), they observed engulfment defects including septal membrane bulges and asymmetric membrane migration — phenotypes consistent with impaired peptidoglycan remodeling. A K30A point mutant, which impairs ATP hydrolysis and filament formation in vitro but retains partial function for coat assembly in vivo, closely recapitulated these engulfment defects. The authors propose that SpoIVA polymerizes at the spore surface to create a cytoskeletal-like structure that mechanically drives phagocytic engulfment, analogous to how eukaryotic cells use actin-based forces during phagocytosis. This work is fundamentally basic research with no immediate commercial application evident but contributes mechanistic insight into bacterial cell biology with potential relevance for understanding spore-forming pathogens and engineering bacterial systems.

### Mutations within and outside the PfFNT transport pathway drive MMV007839 resistance in Plasmodium falciparum
**Priority:** high | **Tags:** antimalarial, drug-resistance, parasitic-disease, structural-biology
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.08.737153v1

Researchers at the Australian National University identified two novel resistance-conferring mutations (V200L and L198M) in PfFNT, a formate-nitrite transporter essential for lactate/H+ export in Plasmodium falciparum. These mutations, discovered through in vitro evolution experiments with the PfFNT inhibitor MMV007839, produced 6-fold and 13-fold increases in IC50 respectively. Using molecular dynamics simulations and thermodynamic integration, the team demonstrated that these mutations—alongside previously characterized resistance mutations from prolonged drug exposure—destabilize inhibitor binding within the substrate transport pathway. The study further predicts additional mutation sites that may enable future resistance to PfFNT-targeted antimalarials. This work has direct implications for antimalarial drug design and predictive resistance profiling.

### A polarity-controlled Tad nanomachine enables prey invasion in a bacterial predator
**Priority:** medium | **Tags:** Microbiology, Synthetic Biology, Bacterial Physiology
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.08.737194v1

Research from UCLouvain's de Duve Institute and APC Microbiome Ireland reveals how the obligate predator Bdellovibrio bacteriovorus uses its tight adherence (Tad) machinery to invade prey. Using CRISPR interference combined with live-cell and microfluidics imaging, the team shows Tad is essential for prolonged prey attachment and subsequent invasion. The machinery assembles at the invasive cell pole before prey encounter, with localization controlled by the polarity hub protein RomR—an atypical TadZA fusion ATPase that physically interacts with RomR to link polarity control to Tad assembly. Published July 8, 2026 (preprint), this work was funded by ERC Consolidator Grant VAMPIRE and other EU/WEL sources. For biotech operators in biocontrol and synthetic biology, understanding how predatory bacteria spatiotemporally regulate their invasion machinery informs engineering of living therapeutics and design principles for nanoscale molecular machines.

### Response Surface Methodology for Melanin Nanoparticle Production Optimization from Producer Strain Pseudomonas stutzeri BTCZ305 with In Vitro Anti-Inflammatory and Wound Healing Potential
**Priority:** low | **Tags:** biomanufacturing, nanotechnology, biomaterials, microbial products
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.08.737209v1

Researchers at Cochin University of Science and Technology optimized the production of melanin nanoparticles using bacterial strain Pseudomonas stutzeri BTCZ 305 through response surface methodology. Employing a Box-Behnken experimental design with seventeen fermentation condition combinations, the team identified optimal parameters of 32.5°C temperature, pH 8.5, and L-tyrosine concentration at 7 g/L, achieving melanin yields of 1192.27 microg/mL as predicted by a quadratic regression model. The resulting nanoparticles were characterized for nanostructural distribution, colloidal stability, and particle size using transmission electron microscopy, particle size analysis, and zeta potential measurements. Preliminary in vitro assays demonstrated anti-inflammatory activity and wound healing potential of the isolated melanin bioproduct.
